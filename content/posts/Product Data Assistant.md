**My Role and What I Do**

<!--more-->

This is a look at what I actually do at SGS Engineering — how my role works and why I enjoy it as much as I do.

When I first joined SGS Engineering, everything moved fast. Within a month I'd already picked up the core workflows — product setup, price changes, attributes & facets, and the PIM. It was a steep learning curve, but the good kind: the kind where you feel yourself becoming useful, not just busy.

Since then, my role has grown into something much bigger and more defined. I now handle products end-to-end — from the initial idea to the moment a customer sees them on the website. I work across data, pricing, content, logistics, and commercial strategy.

But the clearest way to understand the breadth of my role is through the thing I’ve become known for internally: **Bundles**.

{{< figure src="/portfolio/images/Modular.png" width="100%" >}}

Bundles tie together every part of my work, and they go far beyond the typical “group of products” you see on a product page. Because of how bundles are structured in the PIM, I’m also responsible for some of the highest-value areas of SGS’s business — especially the Modular Workshop range, which represents a major share of company profit. Even though these units appear on the website as single products, their modular nature means they’re still treated internally as bundles, with all the complexity that comes with that.

Recently I’ve been building hundreds of new combinations to support upcoming launches — including new components like power-strip-integrated pegboards and other additions to the SWS ecosystem. This work isn’t just administrative; it shapes how customers discover, compare, and ultimately buy large workshop systems. It also ensures our internal structure, pricing hierarchy, and commercial logic stay tight as the range expands.


## Spotting Opportunities

Every bundle starts long before it exists in the PIM. The real work begins in the data.

I analyse what’s selling, what isn’t, where customers expect added value, and where the range feels thin or inconsistent. This is part sales analysis, part pattern recognition, and part understanding how customers actually shop on the website.

I look for things like:

- Gaps in the range — missing sizes, missing combinations, or modules that customers clearly want to pair together
- High-performing products that would convert even better when bundled
- Slow movers that become more attractive when paired with complementary items
- Customer behaviour signals from Clarity and analytics showing where people hesitate, compare, or drop off
- Inconsistencies in pricing or hierarchy that a bundle can simplify

Sometimes the insight comes from numbers — a spike in sales, a dip, or a pattern across similar SKUs. Other times it comes from the structure of the range itself: a product that feels “orphaned”, a module that should logically sit with others, or a gap that becomes obvious once you map the ecosystem.

It’s a mix of detective work and commercial instinct - and eventually a pattern emerges.

## Composition & Validation

Once I’ve identified an opportunity, I build an initial version of the bundle — a working model that defines the components, the structure, and the logic behind how it should function. This gives me something tangible to test.

From here, composition and validation run in parallel. I move back and forth between the technical build and the commercial reality, refining both until the bundle is structurally sound, commercially viable, and strategically useful.

#### Shaping the Structure

I refine the component mix and configuration rules to ensure the bundle is:

- *Structurally coherent*
- *Technically accurate*
- *Easy to understand*
- *Consistent within the wider range*

This stage is iterative — I adjust, test, and re-balance until the bundle feels intuitive and purposeful.


#### Checking Commercial Viability

As the structure evolves, I test it against the commercial checkpoints:

- *Stock Levels* - confirming availability
- *Expected Delivery* - especially for new or low-stock-items
- *Cost Prices* - confirming the margin foundations
- *Bundle Pricing* - calculating the combined pricing based on components
- *Margin* - checking both full-price and discounted scenarios
- *Seasonal/PROMO Priorities* - aligning with trading plans and campaign windows

A bundle that looks good on paper may still be rejected if it cannibalises a hero product or creates confusion. Conversely, a tighter‑margin bundle may still be worth building if it serves a strategic purpose.

#### Cross-Team Review

Once the specification is stable, I present it either directly to my managers or in the bi‑weekly cross‑team meeting I run (Ecommerce, Buying, Line Managers, Sales, PIM). This is where the bundle is challenged from technical, operational, and commercial angles.

Only once everyone is aligned do I move into the system build.

## Building the Bundles

Once I’ve been given the all-clear, I move into the PIM to build the bundle as a structured product. This is where the planning becomes reality — and accuracy matters more than anything else.

This stage is about execution, not decision‑making.
Everything must be built correctly so the bundle behaves exactly as intended on the website.

This includes:

- *Taxonomy Alignment* - ensuring the bundle sits in the correct category structure
- *Dimension & Weight* - confirming accurate logistics data so delivery methods, surcharges, and shipping rules calculate correctly 
- *Titles & Descriptions* - clear, consistent, and SEO-aligned
- *Attributes Mapping* - making sure technical data flows cleanly to the website
- *Component linking* - connecting all SKUs that make up the bundle

This stage is meticulous. A single incorrect attribute or missing link can break facets, hide the product from filters, or cause stock discrepancies. For complex ranges like SWS, where a single unit can contain dozens of components, accuracy is everything.

{{< figure src="/portfolio/images/sws_building.png" width="100%" >}}

I also enter the product into our accounting software. While it doesn’t hold the same level of detail as the PIM, it’s essential for stock management, internal reporting, and ensuring Buying can track active inventory.

Once the data is in place, I send the spreadsheet to Graphic Design — often including reference imagery — so they can create new assets. It may include callout graphics, lifestyle imagery etc. These assets are crucial for conversion. A bundle can be commercially perfect, but without strong imagery, it won’t perform.


## The Web

This stage is where the technical build meets real-world customer behaviour — and small details can make a big difference to visibility and conversion.

I check that the bundle has pulled through correctly to the front end, ensuring:

- Facets are mapped accurately
- The product appears on the correct category page
- It's pinned or prioritised where relevant
- I link similar or complementary products
- I collaborate with Ecommerce to feature it in banners or promotional placement

This stage is about making sure the bundle is not only live but discoverable, logical, and positioned to convert.

{{< figure src="/portfolio/images/SWSG.png" width="100%" >}}

Sometimes a product pulls through correctly, but the facets don’t surface it where customers expect. For example, I’ve had bundles appear on the category page but not show up when filtering by drawer count. After investigating, I found that one inherited attribute wasn’t mapped correctly from a component SKU. Fixing the mapping immediately improved visibility and traffic.

## Tracking


After launch, I monitor performance and make continuous improvements. I use Microsoft Clarity and the built‑in web reports to understand how customers interact with the product page — where they scroll, what they click, how long they stay, and how they navigate to the listing.

I track:

- Customer behaviour
- Traffic sources
- Conversion indicators
- Early sales performance

{{< figure src="/portfolio/images/remarkable.png" width="100%" >}}

This data helps me refine the product over time. Sometimes the improvements are small — adjusting image order, tightening a title, or improving the first paragraph of the description. Other times they’re structural.

For example, with the new SWS components, I noticed customers weren’t scrolling far enough to reach the product callout section. It was positioned too low on the page and wasn’t treated as a primary asset. I updated the layout so the callout became a main visual element — improving visibility, engagement, and overall clarity.

Tracking isn’t a one‑off task; it’s a feedback loop. Each insight feeds back into the product, the category, or even the wider range strategy. It’s how I make sure the bundles I build don’t just go live — they perform.

## Summary

Although bundles aren’t the only part of my job — and I’m always juggling a wide range of tasks — they highlight every aspect of my role:

- *Data Analysis*
- *Sales Insights*
- *Pricing Logic*
- *PIM expertise*
- *Cross-team Collaboration*
- *E-commerce Optimisation*
- *Continuous Improvement*

They’re the clearest example of how I take an idea, extract the data, structure it, build it, launch it, and then track and refine it. It’s the full lifecycle — and one of the parts of my role I’m most proud of.

I’ve genuinely landed well at SGS Engineering. I’m happy with the work I’m doing and the opportunities it offers. Sales are up, management is capable and enthusiastic, and meetings actually lead to decisions and progress. It’s an exciting environment to be part of, and I’m glad to be contributing to it.

What I enjoy most is that my work has a direct impact — on the website, on the customer journey, and on the commercial performance of the business. It’s a role that keeps growing with me, and I’m excited to see where it goes next.
